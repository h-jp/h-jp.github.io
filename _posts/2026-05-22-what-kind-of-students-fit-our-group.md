Chinese title: 什么样的学生适合加入我们的研究组

Research groups have different cultures. Some groups are more theory-oriented, some are more engineering-oriented, and some focus on application deployment. Our group works at the intersection of artificial intelligence, intelligent software engineering, multi-agent systems, and intelligent robotics. This means our projects usually require both research thinking and system-building ability. A student who fits the group does not need to know everything before joining, but should be willing to learn across these boundaries.

The first quality I value is sustained curiosity. Research often begins with a simple question, but the answer is rarely simple. For example, if an LLM-generated program fails a test, we can ask many deeper questions. Did the requirement contain ambiguity? Did the model choose the wrong algorithm? Did the test miss an important behavior? Can runtime traces reveal the failure cause? Can another agent repair the code without breaking a previous case? A curious student does not stop at the first explanation. They keep asking what evidence is available and what remains uncertain.

The second quality is willingness to build. In our directions, ideas usually need to become working systems. A paper about intelligent requirements engineering needs agents, artifacts, prompts, knowledge bases, and evaluation cases. A paper about code generation or repair needs benchmarks, execution environments, logging, test harnesses, and failure analysis. A robotics project needs perception, planning, control, and interaction workflows. Building systems is sometimes frustrating, but it is also where research becomes concrete. Students who enjoy turning ideas into tools will grow quickly.

The third quality is respect for rigor. AI systems can produce impressive demonstrations, but research cannot rely only on demonstrations. We need controlled comparisons, clear evaluation protocols, ablation studies, and careful error analysis. We also need to be honest about limitations. A good result is not only a high score. It is a result that helps others understand what changed, why it changed, and under what conditions the conclusion is valid. This kind of rigor is a habit that students can develop through repeated practice.

The fourth quality is communication. Research is collaborative. Students need to explain their ideas in group meetings, write progress reports, discuss paper drafts, and respond to feedback. Clear communication does not mean speaking fluently from the beginning. It means being willing to make thinking visible. A short note that records the problem, the experiment, the result, and the next question is often more valuable than a long but unclear report.

Students who hope to join should be prepared for a real learning process. There will be paper reading, programming, debugging, experiments, writing, revision, and presentation. There may also be failed ideas and experiments that do not support the original hypothesis. This is normal. The important question is whether we can learn from the failure and improve the next version of the work.

The group is especially suitable for students interested in large language models, multi-agent systems, intelligent software engineering, code generation and self-repair, intelligent requirements engineering, and intelligent robotics. It is also suitable for students who want to strengthen both research ability and engineering ability during graduate study. Some students may later pursue a Ph.D.; others may enter high-level R&D platforms. The common foundation is the same: disciplined learning, careful system building, and the ability to explain technical work with evidence.

I do not expect incoming students to already be mature researchers. I do expect initiative, honesty, patience, and a willingness to do hard work carefully. A good research group should help students grow, but growth also requires active participation from the student. If these expectations sound reasonable rather than intimidating, our group may be a good fit.
